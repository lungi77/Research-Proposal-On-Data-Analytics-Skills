# Developing Your Research Proposal: Data Analytics & Data Science

### Your Document Layout Checklist
To keep the presentation sharp, professional, and readable for the academic review panel, configure your word processor with these baseline rules before you write:
*   **Typography:** Arial font, set to 12pt size.
*   **Spacing & Layout:** Maintain a comfortable 1.5 line spacing with clean 2cm margins on all sides.
*   **Alignment:** Fully justify your text blocks to give it a polished, clean-cut finish.
*   **Visual Structure:** Be consistent with your formatting throughout. Lean on clear headings and subheadings to break up complex ideas into bite-sized, logical sections.

---

## 1. Title
Your title shouldn't just be a placeholder; it needs to be an accurate, descriptive summary of your project. Make sure it explicitly highlights your data analytics approach and the real-world business, engineering, or technical environment you are investigating.

## 2. Introduction & The Core Problem
*   **Setting the Stage:** Introduce your data science domain. What industry, trend, or operational environment are you looking at? 
*   **The Bottleneck:** Pinpoint the exact problem. Is it an architectural bottleneck, a lack of predictive accuracy, or an underutilized data stream?
*   **Why It Matters:** Draft a clear statement of problem relevance. Explain why this issue deserves a dedicated study and what your project intends to solve.
*   **Clear Goals:** Spell out your core research questions alongside measurable objectives or hypotheses. 

## 3. Literature Review (Mapping the Field)
This is where you show you know your stuff. Dive into the existing research, technical frameworks, and analytical models surrounding your problem space.
*   Discuss the foundational algorithms, seminal studies, and modern methodologies (e.g., machine learning architectures, deep networks, or optimization loops) that form your baseline.
*   Critique the existing landscape to justify your own architectural choices.
*   Look for the gaps. Highlight exactly where prior research stalls, lacks scale, or overlooks a variable, and show how your project bridges that divide.

## 4. Proposed Methodology & Pipeline
Walk the reader through your technical blueprint. Treat this section like a logical data engineering and modeling pipeline:
*   **Data Ingestion & Extraction:** Where is your data living? Detail your targets (APIs, web scraping, production databases, or open repositories) and map out how you plan to ingest it securely and legally.
*   **Data Preprocessing & Preparation:** Real-world data is dirty. Outline your engineering strategy for handling missing values, scaling features, transforming variables, or handling class imbalances.
*   **Modeling & Execution:** Specify the exact algorithms, framework libraries, and analytical environments you intend to build with.
*   **The 'Why':** Don't just list tools. Justify why this specific pipeline is the right choice for your specific dataset, referencing technical literature to back up your engineering decisions.

## 5. Data Governance & Ethical Guardrails
Data projects carry unique responsibilities. Outline your commitment to clean, responsible engineering:
*   **Privacy & Identity:** How will you scrub personal identifiers and protect anonymity?
*   **Security & Storage:** Describe your storage architecture, access privileges, and data retention policies.
*   **Responsible AI:** Address potential algorithm bias, dataset fairness, transparency, model interpretability, and pipeline accountability.

## 6. Real-World Constraints & Risk Management
No engineering plan is perfect. Call out the limitations you expect to encounter over the project lifecycle:
*   Look ahead at compute limitations, data velocity barriers, storage costs, or restricted data access.
*   Propose realistic mitigation strategies to ensure your project stays on track even when infrastructure or data pipelines throw a curveball.

## 7. Expected Impact & Outcomes
What happens when your project succeeds? Define the tangible value your work leaves behind:
*   **Academic Value:** New technical insights, modified architectures, or optimization benchmarks.
*   **Practical Deliverables:** Clean code repositories, production-ready models, interactive analytical dashboards, or actionable data-driven insights.

## 8. Development Roadmap & Milestones
Break your semester-long development lifecycle into clear, logical phases. Use crisp milestones—like *Pipeline configuration finalized*, *Feature engineering complete*, *Model evaluation optimized*—to demonstrate that your proposal is practical, achievable, and well-paced.

## Academic Referencing Guidelines

### Setting Up Zotero for Seamless Citations
Don't write your references manually. Automate the heavy lifting using Zotero:
1. Download the **Zotero Desktop app** and install the browser connector extension.
2. In Zotero, navigate to `Edit > Preferences > Export` and select **Harvard** as your default output formatting style.
3. Organize your research early: create collection folders matching your proposal structure (e.g., *Methodology Studies*, *Domain Background*).
4. Use the word processor plugin to drop in citations while you type, allowing Zotero to compile a flawless bibliography automatically at the bottom of your file.

### Harvard Style Citation Cheat Sheet

#### Writing In-Text Citations
*   **Single Author:** Recent predictive modeling indicates that feature selection is paramount (Smith, 2024).
*   **Two Authors:** Operational efficiency scales when data pipelines are containerized (Smith and Jones, 2025).
*   **Three or More Authors:** Implementing real-time inference clusters requires robust structural data governance (Brown *et al.*, 2026).

#### Organizing Your Reference List
*   **Books:**  
    Author, A.A. (Year) *Title of book in italics*. Edition (if not the 1st). Place of publication: Publisher.
*   **Journal Articles & Papers:**  
    Author, B.B., Author, C.C. and Author, D.D. (Year) 'Title of journal article', *Title of Journal in Italics*, Volume(Issue), pp. Page numbers.
*   **Web Documentation & Reports:**  
    Author, E.E. (Year) *Title of document or technical page in italics*. Available at: URL (Accessed: Day Month Year).
